**Overview**: 
Through this project, our group, JEAwesome, examines whether lower income neighborhoods in Chicago have reduced access to grocery stores compared to higher income areas. Limited access to grocery stores can contribute to food insecurity and health disparities, making it an important issue in urban planning and public health. 
To further investigate this question, we acquired two datasets: one from the City of Chicago, the Grocery Store Status Map, and another from the U.S. Census. The Grocery Store Status dataset provides geographic information such as zip codes and coordinates of grocery stores, while the Census dataset provides demographic data, including income and location information.
After cleaning and preparing both datasets, we plan to merge them using a shared geographic identifier (zip code). We will then calculate the density of grocery stores per zip code or neighborhood and analyze how this relates to income levels.
Ultimately, our goal is to provide a data driven perspective on food accessibility in Chicago and highlight areas where interventions may be needed to improve equitable access to grocery stores.

**Constraints**: 
Regarding the Census data, it is known that many individuals chose not to participate, which may lead to an incomplete or inaccurate representation of demographics. Furthermore, the dataset was collected in 2020, and while the income figures have been adjusted for inflation, they may not fully capture the current economic realities in Chicago. Changes in housing costs, employment patterns, and local development over the past few years could mean that the data does not accurately reflect today’s income conditions. As a result, any conclusions drawn should be considered within the context of these limitations.  Additionally, since our project focuses solely on Chicago, the findings may not generalize to other cities. As for the grocery store dataset, it was last updated in June 2025 (some grocery stores not being updated since 2020!) so it may not reflect the most current information, potentially introducing discrepancies compared to present day conditions. Another concern is that the datasets are not individualized and over generalized. The Census data categorizes individuals based on factors such as location, socioeconomic status, marital status, and housing. Because of this, we must be cautious not to make broad generalizations about a neighborhood or area solely based on its ZIP code, and we should keep in mind that there might be potential diversity within these groups. In our case, we have to keep in mind that there might be some with lower or higher income (there might be outliers that are hidden beneath the median) compared to the median income of that specified zip code.
